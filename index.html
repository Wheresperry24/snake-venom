Introduction

What is Snake Venom?

Snake venom is a highly modified form of saliva: a complex mixture of proteins, peptides, enzymes, and non-protein molecules produced by specialized glands. It has evolved over millions of years to subdue prey, aid digestion, and serve as a defense mechanism.

🧪

A Biochemical Cocktail

Venom is not a single substance: it's a complex mixture containing 20 to over 100 distinct proteins and peptides. These molecules work synergistically: some paralyze nerve signaling, others destroy cell membranes or prevent blood from clotting. The exact composition varies dramatically between species, populations, and even individual snakes, making each venom a unique biochemical fingerprint shaped by millions of years of natural selection.

⚗️

Dry Weight Composition

Approximately 90–95% of venom's dry weight consists of proteins and polypeptides. The remainder includes free amino acids, nucleotides, carbohydrates, lipids, and metallic ions like zinc and calcium.

🔬

Evolutionary Origins

Venom proteins evolved from normal body proteins through gene duplication. For example, phospholipase A₂, a digestive enzyme, was recruited into venom glands where it evolved potent toxic properties.

🐍

Delivery System

Venom is synthesized in modified parotid (salivary) glands and delivered through hollow or grooved fangs. Muscle contractions around the gland control the volume injected. Snakes can even deliver "dry bites" with no venom.

Toxin Families

Key Components

Snake venom toxins are classified by their molecular targets and biological effects. Here are the major families found across venomous species:

01

Neurotoxins

Target the nervous system by blocking neuromuscular transmission. α-Bungarotoxin from kraits binds irreversibly to nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChR), causing paralysis and respiratory failure.

Target: Nicotinic AChR
Elapidae Paralysis
02

Phospholipases A₂ (PLA₂)

Catalyze the hydrolysis of phospholipids in cell membranes at the sn-2 position. This destroys cell integrity, releases arachidonic acid, and triggers inflammation. PLA₂ enzymes require Ca²⁺ as a cofactor.

Reaction: Phospholipid → Lysophospholipid + Fatty Acid
Ca²⁺ Dependent Hemolysis
03

Metalloproteinases (SVMPs)

Zinc-dependent enzymes that degrade extracellular matrix proteins including collagen and fibronectin. Cause hemorrhage, tissue necrosis, and edema by destroying blood vessel walls and basement membranes.

Cofactor: Zn²⁺ (catalytic site)
Viperidae Hemorrhagic
04

Serine Proteases

Disrupt the blood coagulation cascade by mimicking or inhibiting clotting factors such as thrombin and Factor X. Some activate prothrombin while others are anticoagulant, leading to uncontrolled bleeding or disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).

Mechanism: Catalytic triad (His-Asp-Ser)
Coagulopathy DIC
05

Three-Finger Toxins (3FTx)

Small proteins (~60–74 amino acids) with a characteristic three β-strand loop structure stabilized by 4–5 disulfide bonds. They target diverse receptors — from ion channels to muscarinic receptors — and are the dominant component of elapid venoms.

Structure: 3 β-loops, 4–5 S–S bonds
Elapidae Multi-target
06

L-Amino Acid Oxidases (LAAOs)

Flavoenzymes that catalyze the oxidative deamination of L-amino acids, producing H₂O₂ (hydrogen peroxide) as a byproduct. The H₂O₂ induces apoptosis, edema, and has antibacterial and antiparasitic effects.

Byproduct: H₂O₂ + NH₃ + α-keto acid
FAD Cofactor Cytotoxic

PDB ID1H5O
Residues42 amino acids
Mass~4,880 Da

A small, basic myotoxin (pI ~10.3) that targets voltage-gated sodium channels (Nav1.4) in skeletal muscle, causing spastic paralysis (distinct from the flaccid paralysis of neurotoxins). Structurally similar to β-defensins. How Venom Attacks

1

Injection & Spreading

Venom is injected through hollow fangs into subcutaneous tissue. Hyaluronidase, the "spreading factor", breaks down hyaluronic acid in the extracellular matrix, allowing toxins to rapidly diffuse through tissues and enter the bloodstream.

Spreading Factor • Hyaluronidase
2

Neuromuscular Blockade

Neurotoxins reach the neuromuscular junction within minutes. Presynaptic toxins (β-neurotoxins like β-bungarotoxin) destroy nerve terminals by degrading membrane phospholipids. Postsynaptic toxins (α-neurotoxins like cobratoxin) block acetylcholine receptors. Both pathways lead to paralysis.

α-Neurotoxins • β-Neurotoxins
3

Hemostatic Disruption

Serine proteases and metalloproteinases attack the coagulation cascade from multiple angles. Some activate prothrombin (consuming clotting factors → consumptive coagulopathy), while others directly degrade fibrinogen or inhibit platelet aggregation, leading to uncontrollable hemorrhage.

SVMPs • Serine Proteases • Coagulopathy
4

Tissue Destruction

PLA₂ enzymes and cytotoxins destroy cell membranes through enzymatic hydrolysis and pore formation. Metalloproteinases degrade the extracellular matrix. Together, they cause myonecrosis (muscle death), dermonecrosis, and massive local tissue damage that begins at the bite site and spreads.

PLA₂ • Cytotoxins • Necrosis
5

Systemic Organ Failure

As toxins circulate, they target distant organs. Nephrotoxins cause acute kidney injury via myoglobin accumulation. Cardiotoxins disrupt cardiac ion channels. If untreated, the combined assault leads to multi-organ failure — respiratory collapse (from paralysis), renal failure, and cardiovascular shock.

Renal Failure • Cardiotoxicity • Shock
Biomedical Research

Medicine

The same toxic properties that make venom deadly are being harnessed by scientists to develop life-saving treatments and research tools.

💊

Captopril (ACE Inhibitor)

From: Bothrops jararaca

The first ACE inhibitor, developed from bradykinin-potentiating peptides found in pit viper venom. Captopril revolutionized the treatment of hypertension and heart failure. It inhibits angiotensin-converting enzyme, preventing vasoconstriction.

Impact: Used by millions worldwide for blood pressure control
🩸

Eptifibatide (Integrilin)

From: Sistrurus miliarius (Pygmy Rattlesnake)

A synthetic cyclic peptide derived from barbourin, a disintegrin in pygmy rattlesnake venom. It blocks the glycoprotein IIb/IIIa receptor on platelets, preventing aggregation. Used during heart attacks and coronary interventions to prevent blood clots.

Impact: Standard of care in acute coronary syndrome
🧬

Ziconotide (Prialt)

From: Conus magus (Cone Snail)

While from a cone snail rather than a snake, this illustrates venom-to-drug pipelines. A synthetic ω-conotoxin that blocks N-type calcium channels in spinal neurons. Used for severe chronic pain in patients who don't respond to opioids — 1,000× more potent than morphine without addiction risk.

Impact: Non-addictive alternative to opioid painkillers
🛡️

Antivenom Production

Developed since 1895

The primary treatment for envenomation. Produced by hyperimmunizing horses or sheep with sub-lethal doses of venom, then collecting and purifying the antibodies (IgG). Modern antivenoms use F(ab')₂ or Fab fragments to reduce allergic reactions. Next-gen approaches include recombinant human antibodies.

Impact: Saves an estimated 100,000+ lives annually
